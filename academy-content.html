Module 1: Introduction to the UN and MUN

Objective: Understand the United Nations system, different types of committees, and the role and responsibilities of a delegate in MUN.

Lesson 1: Understanding the United Nations

The United Nations (UN) is an international organization founded in 1945, right after World War II, to prevent future conflicts, promote peace, and create a platform for countries to cooperate on global issues.

  • The UN has 193 member states.
  • It addresses global challenges like climate change, refugees, and pandemics.
  • It provides forums for diplomacy so countries negotiate instead of resorting to war.
The Six Main Organs of the UN:
1. General Assembly (UNGA): 193 members. One vote each. Non-binding resolutions.
2. Security Council (UNSC): 15 members. 5 Permanent (US, UK, FR, CH, RU). Binding resolutions.
3. ECOSOC: Coordinates economic and social programs (WHO, UNICEF).
4. ICJ: Settles legal disputes between countries.
5. Secretariat: Administrative body led by the Secretary-General.
6. Trusteeship Council: Currently inactive.
Tip: UNSC resolutions can be enforced; UNGA resolutions carry international moral weight but are not binding.

Lesson 2: Types of MUN Committees

  • GA Committees: Large (100+ delegates). Focus on policy-making.
  • Specialized Agencies: Technical (WHO, UNICEF, UNDP).
  • Crisis Committees: Fast-paced, real-time scenarios.

Module 2: Rules of Procedure (RoP)

Objective: Learn essential procedures, motions, points, and voting.

Lesson 2: Motions

  • Motion to Open Debate: Begin discussion on the agenda.
  • Moderated Caucus: Short, timed speeches on subtopics.
  • Unmoderated Caucus: Informal discussion for negotiating and drafting.
  • Motion to Table the Topic: Postpone debate temporarily.

Lesson 3: Points

Point of Order: Correct procedural mistakes.
Point of Personal Privilege: Issues like "I cannot hear the speaker."
Point of Information: Ask a question to a speaker.
Point of Inquiry: Ask the chair a procedural question.

Lesson 5: Voting & Amendments

Friendly Amendment: Supported by all sponsors; added directly.
Unfriendly Amendment: Not supported by all; requires a vote.

Module 3: Public Speaking and Diplomacy

Objective: Build confidence, master rhetoric, and professional conduct.

Lesson 1: Structure of a Speech

  1. Introduction: Greet the chair and introduce country/topic.
  2. Body: Present arguments logically using facts and data.
  3. Conclusion: Summarize stance and suggest actionable steps.

Lesson 2: Persuasive Language

Use evidence, appeal to shared interests, and use inclusive language.
Replace "You must" with "The delegation encourages..."

Exercise: Rewrite "You are wrong, your policy will fail" into diplomatic form.

Module 4: Research and Position Papers

Lesson 2: Country Research

Research your political system, national policies, and international alliances. Identify Priorities (Non-negotiable) vs. Negotiable points.

Lesson 4: Writing a Position Paper

  • Header: Committee, Topic, Country.
  • Background: Context and history of the issue.
  • Country Policy: Your initiatives and stance.
  • Proposed Solutions: Concrete actions.

Module 5: Resolution Writing

Lesson 2: Structure

Preambulatory Clauses: Describe the background (starts with Recalling, Recognizing, Deeply concerned).

Operative Clauses: Actionable solutions (starts with Encourages, Urges, Requests).

Sponsors draft the resolution; Signatories support the discussion of it.

Module 6: Negotiation and Lobbying

Lesson 2: Forming Blocs

Regional Blocs: (African Union, EU).
Interest-based Blocs: Countries with shared policy goals.

Lesson 4: Compromise Techniques

  • Concede minor points while keeping core interests.
  • Bundle clauses to include multiple proposals.
  • Use friendly amendments to win supporters.
