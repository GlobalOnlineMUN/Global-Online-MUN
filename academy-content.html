Module 1: Introduction to the UN and MUN

Objective: Understand the United Nations system, different types of committees, and the role and responsibilities of a delegate in MUN.

Lesson 1: Understanding the United Nations

What is the UN?

The United Nations (UN) is an international organization founded in 1945, right after World War II, to prevent future conflicts, promote peace, and create a platform for countries to cooperate on global issues. Its mission is not only to maintain international peace, but also to advance human rights, economic development, and humanitarian aid worldwide.

Key points to remember:

  • The UN has 193 member states.
  • It addresses global challenges like climate change, refugees, conflicts, and pandemics.
  • The UN provides forums for diplomacy so that countries can negotiate instead of resorting to war.
The Six Main Organs of the UN
  1. General Assembly (UNGA): Composed of all 193 member states. Each country has one vote. Focus: discuss global issues, pass non-binding resolutions. Example: The UNGA may debate climate action.
  2. Security Council (UNSC): 15 members: 5 permanent (US, UK, France, China, Russia) and 10 rotating. Passes binding resolutions regarding peace and security.
  3. Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC): Coordinates international economic and social programs. Works with WHO, UNICEF, and UNDP.
  4. International Court of Justice (ICJ): Settles legal disputes between countries.
  5. Secretariat: The UN’s administrative body led by the Secretary-General.
  6. Trusteeship Council: Currently inactive.
Tip for delegates: Always know the difference between binding and non-binding resolutions. UNSC resolutions can be enforced; UNGA resolutions cannot, but they carry international moral weight.
Lesson 2: Types of MUN Committees
  • General Assembly Committees: Large committees (100+ delegates). Focus: policy-making. Example: Third Committee (Human Rights).
  • Security Council (UNSC): Small committee (15 delegates). Decisions are binding.
  • Specialized Agencies: Focused, technical committees like WHO, UNICEF, and UNDP.
  • Crisis Committees: Fast-paced simulations. Delegates act quickly and draft resolutions in real time.
Exercise: Research one committee (UNSC, UNGA, or WHO) and write 3 bullet points describing its function.
Lesson 3: Role of a Delegate
  1. Research: Study country policies, allies, and context.
  2. Position Papers: Write formal documents summarizing your country’s stance.
  3. Debate & Negotiation: Speak in committee, form alliances, and negotiate.
  4. Diplomacy: Act professionally and respect opposing viewpoints.
Example: Country: Egypt | Topic: Climate Change
"Egypt recognizes the urgent need for renewable energy in Africa and encourages member states to contribute..."
Lesson 4: MUN Terms You Must Know
  • Delegate: Student representing a country.
  • Resolution: Formal proposal drafted to solve an issue.
  • Motion: Formal request for a specific action.
  • Point: Procedural question or objection.
  • Caucus: Short break to negotiate or strategize.

Module 2: Rules of Procedure (RoP)

Objective: Learn essential MUN procedures, including motions, points, debate flow, voting, and amendments.

Lesson 1: Introduction to RoP

Rules govern how debates are conducted, how decisions are made, and how delegates interact. It ensures orderly debate and gives delegates fair opportunity to speak.

Lesson 2: Motions
  • Motion to Open Debate: Begin discussion on the topic.
  • Motion to Close Debate: End discussion and move to voting.
  • Motion for Moderated Caucus: Short, timed speeches on a subtopic.
  • Motion for Unmoderated Caucus: Informal discussion for negotiating and drafting.
  • Motion to Adjourn/End Session: End the committee session.
  • Motion to Table the Topic: Postpone debate temporarily.
Lesson 3: Points
  • Point of Order: Correct procedural mistakes.
  • Point of Personal Privilege: Raise personal issues (e.g., volume).
  • Point of Information: Ask a question to a speaker.
  • Point of Inquiry: Ask the chair procedural questions.
  • Right of Reply: Respond to personal attacks.
Lesson 4: Debate Flow

Sequence:

  • 1. Roll Call -> Chair confirms attendance of all delegates.
  • 2. Speakers’ List -> Delegates request to speak; ordered by the chair.
  • 3. Formal Debate -> Delegates give speeches following the speaker's list.
  • 4. Moderated Caucus -> Timed short speeches on a subtopic.
  • 5. Unmoderated Caucus -> Informal negotiation and drafting.
  • 6. Resolution Writing -> Drafting and proposing resolutions.
  • 7. Voting -> Approving resolutions or amendments.

    • Lesson 5: Voting Procedures
    • Roll Call Vote: Each country votes Yes, No, or Abstain.
    • Simple Majority: >50% needed.
    • Two-Thirds Majority: Required for critical resolutions (e.g., sanctions).
    • Division of the Question: Separates clauses for individual voting.
    Lesson 6: Amendments
    • Friendly: Supported by all sponsors; added directly.
    • Unfriendly: Not supported by all; requires a vote.
    Exercise: Take this clause: “Urges member states to plant trees globally.” Propose a friendly amendment that adds a measurable goal.

    Module 3: Public Speaking and Diplomacy

    Objective: Build confidence in public speaking, master rhetoric, and develop professional diplomatic conduct.

    Public speaking is the core of MUN. It is not just about talking; it is about persuading other delegates to support your country’s position.

    Lesson 1: The Foundations of Public Speaking Structure of a Speech
    1. <Introduction: Greet the chair and delegates formally. State your country’s overall stance.
    2. Body: Present your arguments logically. Use facts, statistics, and examples. Focus on 2-3 main points.
    3. Conclusion: Summarize your stance and suggest actionable steps. End with a call to action or a formal closing.
    Lesson 2: Persuasive Language and Rhetoric

    How you say something is as important as what you say.

    • Evidence-Based: Use data to back your claims.
    • Shared Interests: Appeal to common goals (e.g., "It is in the interest of all nations to protect the oceans").
    • Inclusive Language: Use "we," "us," and "our" to build unity.
    • Diplomatic Phrasing: Replace "must" with "encourage," "propose," or "suggest."
    Exercise: Rewrite the following sentences to be more diplomatic:
    1. "You are wrong about your climate policy."
    2. "Our country will not accept any compromise on this."
    3. "You must follow our proposal because it is the best."
    Lesson 3: Nonverbal Communication
    • Body Language: Stand tall, use purposeful hand gestures, and avoid crossing your arms.
    • Eye Contact: Look at different parts of the room, not just your paper.
    • Tone and Pace: Speak clearly and vary your tone to emphasize key points. Avoid speaking too fast.
    Lesson 4: Diplomatic Conduct and Etiquette
    • Respect: Always respect other delegates, even if you disagree.
    • No Personal Attacks: Critique the policy, not the person.
    • Active Listening: Take notes during other speeches so you can respond effectively.
    Outcome: By the end of this module, delegates will be able to structure speeches effectively, use persuasive language, and maintain professional decorum.

    Module 4: Research and Position Papers

    Objective: Teach delegates how to research their country, develop a stance, and write professional position papers.

    Lesson 1: The Importance of Research

    Research is the foundation of a successful delegate. Without it, you cannot accurately represent your country or propose realistic solutions.

    Lesson 2: Conducting Effective Research
    • Country Profile: Political system, economy, geography, and culture.
    • International Alliances: Which regional groups (EU, AU, ASEAN) does your country belong to?
    • Past UN Resolutions: How has your country voted on this topic in the past?
    • Topic Context: What are the historical and current facts surrounding the issue?
    Lesson 3: Developing a Country Stance

    Use a "Priority Matrix":

    • Priority 1: Non-negotiable points.
    • Priority 2: Negotiable points where you can compromise.
    • Priority 3: Secondary points that are less critical.
    Lesson 4: Writing a Position Paper
    1. Header: Committee, Topic, Country, and Delegate Name.
    2. Background of the Topic: Briefly summarize the issue with historical context.
    3. Country Policy: Explain your country’s past actions and current priorities.
    4. Proposed Solutions: Suggest 3-4 concrete, realistic actions.
    5. Conclusion: Reaffirm your commitment to international cooperation.
    Exercise:strong> Write a one-paragraph position paper summary for your assigned country and topic. Ensure it includes one fact and one proposed solution.

    Module 5: Resolution Writing

    Objective: Teach delegates how to draft formal UN resolutions, including preambulatory and operative clauses.

    Lesson 1: What is a Resolution?

    A resolution is a formal document that proposes solutions to the topic being debated. It is the final "product" of a committee.

    Lesson 2: Structure of a Resolution
    1. Heading: Committee name and topic.
    2. Preambulatory Clauses: Describe the background and why the resolution is necessary.
      • Starts with words like: Recalling, Recognizing, Alarmed by, Deeply concerned.
    3. Operative Clauses: List the specific actions to be taken.
      • Starts with words like: Encourages, Urges, Requests, Emphasizes.
    Lesson 3: Sponsors and Signatories
    • Sponsors: The primary authors who fully support the resolution.
    • Signatories: Delegates who want to see the resolution debated but may not fully agree with everything in it.
    Lesson 4: Writing Effective Clauses

    Operative clauses must be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).

    Lesson 5: Amendments
    • Friendly Amendment: Supported by all sponsors; added to the resolution without a vote.
    • Unfriendly Amendment: Not supported by all sponsors; requires a vote by the committee.
    Exercise: Draft 2 preambulatory clauses and 2 operative clauses for a resolution on "Improving Access to Clean Water."

    Module 6: Negotiation and Lobbying

    Objective: Teach delegates how to form alliances, lobby for their ideas, and reach compromises during unmoderated caucuses.

    Lesson 1: The Art of Lobbying

    Lobbying happens mostly during unmoderated caucuses. It is the process of talking to other delegates to gain support for your ideas.

    Lesson 2: Forming Blocs
    • Regional Blocs: Based on geography (e.g., African nations, Latin American nations).
    • Interest-based Blocs: Based on shared policy goals (e.g., developed vs. developing nations).
    • Issue-specific Blocs: Temporary alliances formed around a specific part of the resolution.
    Lesson 3: Effective Negotiation Techniques
    • Find Common Ground: Focus on what you agree on first.
    • Be Persuasive, Not Pushy: Use logic and evidence to convince others.
    • Listen: Understand what other countries need so you can find a compromise.
    Lesson 4: Mastering Compromise

    Compromise is essential in diplomacy. You may have to give up a small point to win a larger one. Strategy: "I will support your clause on education if you support my clause on healthcare funding."

    Exercise: Role-play a 2-minute negotiation. Partner A wants a 10% tax on plastic, Partner B wants a 5% tax. Find a middle ground.

    Module 7: MUN Simulation Preparation

    Objective: Combine all skills learned in previous modules to prepare for a full MUN conference simulation.

    Lesson 1: The Conference Checklist
    • Research folder (facts, stats, past resolutions).
    • Finalized Position Paper.
    • Opening Speech (well-practiced).
    • Notepad and pen for note-taking.
    Lesson 2: Day-of-Conference Strategy
    1. Opening: Set a strong tone with your opening speech.
    2. Middle: Be active in moderated caucuses. Pass notes to allies.
    3. Ending: Focus on resolution writing and ensuring your country’s priorities are included.
    Lesson 3: Handling Challenges
    • If you are nervous: Focus on your notes and remember everyone is learning.
    • If you are stuck: Ask the chair for a "Point of Inquiry."
    • If you are outvoted: Stay diplomatic and continue to contribute to the debate.
    Mock Simulation: 1. Conduct a mock roll call. 2. Practice 1-minute opening speeches. 3. Run a 5-minute moderated caucus on a simple topic like "Healthy School Lunches."
